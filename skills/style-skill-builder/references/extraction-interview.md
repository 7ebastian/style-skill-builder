# Extraction Interview

Use this when source material is thin, inconsistent, too polished, or missing examples of failure.

Ask only the questions needed for the current gap. Prefer one cluster at a time.

## Taste Calibration

1. What should this writing feel like when it is working?
2. What should it never feel like, even if the facts are right?
3. Name one writer, publication, or internal example that is directionally close. What part should be borrowed and what part should not?
4. What kind of reader should feel respected by this style?
5. What kind of reader should not be pandered to?

## Structure

1. How should pieces usually start?
2. How quickly should the writing state the main point?
3. Should arguments move from story to idea, idea to example, or another pattern?
4. What makes an ending feel satisfying without becoming summary?

## Sentence-Level Preferences

1. Should the rhythm be spare, conversational, lyrical, reportorial, technical, punchy, or mixed?
2. Are fragments, contractions, first person, direct address, or humor allowed?
3. What words, phrases, or constructions should be cut?
4. What level of jargon is acceptable for the intended reader?

## Examples

Ask for at least one of each:

- A paragraph that sounds right.
- A paragraph that is accurate but wrong for the voice.
- A strong opening.
- A weak opening.
- A good ending.
- A common AI-generated line that should be avoided.

## Constraints

1. Are there legal, compliance, accessibility, or inclusivity rules?
2. Are there product names, spellings, capitalization rules, or word-list entries?
3. Do different channels need different rules?
4. Does the style change for acquisition, onboarding, support, editorial, social, legal, or educational content?

## Minimal Interview Output

When finished, summarize:

- What is confidently known.
- What is plausible but unproven.
- What still needs source material.
- Which decisions should stay as open questions in the guide.
